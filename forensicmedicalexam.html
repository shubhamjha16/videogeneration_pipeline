Concept Explanation: Hinged (Hingee) Fracture of Skull

A "hinged" or "hingee" fracture of the skull is a type of skull fracture where a part of the skull bone is depressed and acts like a hinge, such that one part moves relative to the other, similar to opening and closing a door. This kind of fracture typically involves a linear or comminuted fracture with mobility at the fracture site due to trauma. It is often referred to as a "motorcyclist's fracture" in forensic medicine because of its frequent occurrence in high-velocity injuries, notably in motorcycle accidents. These injuries usually result from a significant impact that transmits force to the skull, causing the bone to crack and act as a movable segment.

Option Analysis

  • A. Car Driver
    Car drivers can experience head injuries in collisions, but the pattern and mechanism of injury (often against a steering wheel or airbag) more commonly result in linear, depressed, or basilar fractures rather than a classic "hinged" fracture.
  • B. Car Passenger
    Like drivers, passengers are at risk of head trauma in car accidents, but the hingee/hinged fracture is rarely described as a specific car passenger injury due to similar mechanisms as the driver.
  • C. Pedestrian
    Pedestrians struck by vehicles can suffer from a variety of skull fractures, depending on how the head strikes the ground or vehicle. However, "hinged" fractures are not characteristically seen in these scenarios.
  • D. Motorcycler driver
    This option is correct. Hinged (hingee) fractures are classically associated with motorcycle accidents. When the rider is thrown from the motorcycle, the head can sustain a severe impact (often with the road or another vehicle), resulting in a high-velocity force that causes a portion of the skull to become mobile, much like a hinge. This is why it is sometimes called the "motorcyclist's fracture" in forensic and trauma references.

Correct Answer

D. Motorcycler driver
