Extradural hemorrhage, or EDH, is a collection of blood between the inner surface of the skull and the dura mater.
It is usually caused by trauma to the head, leading to the rupture of the middle meningeal artery.
EDH is a neurosurgical emergency! The blood increases intracranial pressure and can compress the brain.
Without treatment, this leads to herniation and death. 
The mainstay of management is immediate surgical intervention to evacuate the hematoma as soon as possible.

Let's analyze the management options for Extradural Hemorrhage.
Option A: Antibiotics is wrong. There is no infection here; it is a problem of acute bleeding.
Option B: Immediate evacuation is correct! This is the cornerstone of life-saving management.
Option C: Evacuation after 24 hours is incorrect. Delaying surgery can lead to rapid clinical deterioration.
Option D: Observation is typically reserved for only very small, asymptomatic hematomas.
Therefore, the correct answer is B: Immediate surgical evacuation.
According to Bailey and Love's Textbook of Surgery, EDH is a neurosurgical emergency that requires prompt surgical intervention.
