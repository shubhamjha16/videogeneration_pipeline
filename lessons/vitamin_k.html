Concept Explanation: Vitamin K-Dependent Factors

Vitamin K is an essential nutrient that plays a critical role in the process of blood coagulation (clotting). It is required for the synthesis of several proteins, known as coagulation factors, that are necessary for blood to clot properly. These proteins are synthesized in the liver and require vitamin K for the final modifications that allow them to bind calcium ions, which is essential for their activity in the clotting cascade.

The main vitamin K-dependent coagulation factors are: Factor II (prothrombin), Factor VII, Factor IX, and Factor X. In addition, two regulatory proteins involved in anticoagulation—Protein C and Protein S—are also vitamin K-dependent.

Option Analysis

  • A. II (Prothrombin)
    Correct. Factor II, known as prothrombin, is a vitamin K-dependent coagulation factor. Vitamin K is necessary for its synthesis and function, as it allows for the proper binding of calcium and activation of the clotting cascade.
  • B. III (Tissue Factor)
    Incorrect. Factor III, also known as tissue factor or thromboplastin, is not a vitamin K-dependent factor. It is a cell surface glycoprotein that acts as a cofactor for factor VIIa in the extrinsic pathway of coagulation.
  • C. IV (Calcium)
    Incorrect. Factor IV is calcium, which is an essential mineral for the clotting process but is not synthesized as a protein and does not depend on vitamin K. Calcium ions are required for various steps in the coagulation cascade.
  • D. VI (Not Assigned/No Longer Recognized)
    Incorrect. Factor VI is not recognized as a coagulation factor in humans. It was previously believed to play a role but is now considered obsolete.

Correct Answer

The correct answer is A. II.
