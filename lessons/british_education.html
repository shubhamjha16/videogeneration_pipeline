Evolution of British Indian Education System

The modern education system in India has its roots in the British colonial period. It was a transition from traditional gurukuls and madrasas to a Western-style institutional framework.

1. The Charter Act of 1813

The British East India Company took its first step towards educational responsibility. It sanctioned an annual sum of one lakh rupees for the "revival and improvement of literature" and the promotion of scientific knowledge among the Indians.

2. Macaulay’s Minute (1835)

Lord Macaulay strongly advocated for Western education with English as the medium of instruction. He famously desired to create a class of Indians who were "Indian in blood and colour, but English in taste, in opinions, in morals, and in intellect." This led to the Downward Filtration Theory, which aimed to educate a small upper class who would eventually "filter" knowledge down to the masses.

3. Wood’s Despatch (1854)

Known as the Magna Carta of English Education in India, Sir Charles Wood's plan was the first comprehensive scheme for the spread of education. It recommended:

  • A department of public instruction in every province.
  • Universities in Calcutta, Bombay, and Madras (established 1857).
  • Grants-in-aid to private schools.
  • A focus on secular education and teacher training.

4. Later Commissions

The Hunter Commission (1882) emphasized primary and secondary education, while the Raleigh Commission (1902) led to the Indian Universities Act of 1904, increasing government control over higher education.
