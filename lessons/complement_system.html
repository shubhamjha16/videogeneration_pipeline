Step 1: Concept Explanation

The complement system is a crucial part of the immune system, consisting of a series of proteins that enhance (complement) the ability of antibodies and phagocytic cells to clear pathogens from an organism. C3 is one of the central components of the complement system. Low C3 levels in the blood often signify activation and consumption of complement, typically as a result of immune complex-mediated diseases where immune complexes deposit in tissues, activating the classical complement pathway and consuming complement proteins such as C3.

Diseases that frequently lead to low C3 include those that involve immune complex deposition and complement activation. Persistent low C3 can point toward chronic immune complex diseases, whereas transient low C3 can follow acute episodes.

Step 2: Option Analysis

  • A. Post streptococcal glomerulonephritis:
    This condition usually follows a streptococcal infection and is marked by immune complex deposition in the glomerulus. It commonly leads to decreased C3 levels (often transiently), due to complement activation by the classical pathway.
    Conclusion: Associated with low C3
  • B. Membrano-proliferative glomerulonephritis:
    This is a type of glomerulonephritis characterized by immune complex deposition and persistent activation of the complement system, particularly through the alternative pathway, resulting in consistently low C3 levels.
    Conclusion: Associated with low C3
  • C. Goodpasture’s disease:
    Goodpasture’s disease is mediated by anti-glomerular basement membrane (anti-GBM) antibodies. This is a type II hypersensitivity reaction and does not typically result in immune complex formation or complement activation to the extent that C3 consumption is observed. Thus, C3 levels are usually normal in Goodpasture’s disease.
    Conclusion: Not associated with low C3
  • D. Systemic lupus erythematosus:
    SLE is a prototypical example of an immune complex-mediated disease, frequently leading to complement activation and low levels of complement proteins, particularly during disease flares.
    Conclusion: Associated with low C3

Step 3: Correct Answer

Correct Answer: C. Goodpasture’s disease.
This is because Goodpasture’s disease is caused by anti-GBM antibodies (type II hypersensitivity), and it does not typically result in low C3 levels, unlike the other conditions listed.
